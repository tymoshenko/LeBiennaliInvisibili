The Mayor does not necessarily believe everything The Ambassador tells her when she describes the Biennials visited on her expeditions, but the Mayor does continue listening to the celebrated cultural advisor, and former curator, with greater attention and curiosity than she shows any other cultural attaché or UNESCO representative of hers. In the careers of leaders there is a moment which follows pride in the eclectic line up of the cultural pursuit we have exhibited, and the excitement and fulfillment of knowing we shall soon celebrate any thought of collecting and publicizing them. “Biennials can make or destroy your city, Ma’am” the Ambassador would warn “It is up to you, to choose wisely the alchemy of terms and accents of the gathering you want to create”.

There is a sense of contemplation that comes over us in the morning, with the sound of commuters after eight o’clock and the fumes of traf ic filling streets and squares; a fogginess that makes the buildings and neighborhoods blur on the semi-orthogonal grid of the horizon. From this horizon, a series of curators show up, one after the other, reporting to us the reviews of the last visiting critics, from libel to laudatory, and printouts of the articles of renowned artists who af irm our curatorial production, of ering in exchange periodic submissions of Biennial themes, budget proposals, contact sheets. A sea of tote bags, of memories of chance encounters, of air-kisses, of new names and new zeitgeists.

On this occasion, The Mayor has summoned the Ambassador to report on Biennials from far away lands to prepare her own Mayor’s Biennial. It was through the websites and catalogs destined to be archived, The Mayor started realizing that the construction of realities could be diverse enough to escape the spectator’s conventions. “But, Mrs. Mayor, if one thing you should know about Biennials, it is this: the collaboration between minds and the gathering of spirits has spread as far as beyond our scope and control. What might have begun as a singular cultural event, has spread into myriads of autonomous formations, each of which can seduce and enthrall everyone; even those who never visited, even Biennials that never existed.”

Le Biennali Invisibili

X

Not all biennials are the same. Neither will they return to being what they once were. However, the format of showcasing new work, triggering discussions, and letting new canons emerge, as a time-based event that takes place once every two years at the same location, has become the pilgrimage for the culture industries in the new millennium. Biennials - or biennales, a bastardization of the italian original term that has slipped into as many different languages as locations - not only have the power to forge discourse, but also transient communities united by gatherings every two or three years, and cities that are momentarily transformed and forever associated with those festivals of human culture.

Le Biennali Invisibili is a project that explores eleven imaginaries of such encounters, under the same lens that Italo Calvino created when describing alternative urban realities in his book Le città invisibili (Invisible Cities). Le Biennali Invisibili embraces fiction and pseudo-reality as a framework for curatorial pursuit, as a series of virtual events, proposing and critiquing the conventional celebration of the design & art community.

Le Biennali Invisibili is a project by animali domestici (Alicia Lazzaroni and Antonio Bernacchi), Tijn van de Wijdeven, and Eduardo Cassina. It is curated by Alfons Hug and commissioned by the Goethe-Zentrum Baku.
Alfons Hug is director of Goethe-Zentrum Baku and curator. He has worked extensively in South America, Africa as well as Asia.

Alicia Lazzaroni and Antonio Bernacchi are the co-founders of Animali Domestici, a design practice with an active interest in non anthropocentric coexistence and ecology in its wider sense. They hold a postgraduate master from ETSAM UPM Madrid. After practicing a few years in Singapore, they became instructors and year coordinators in INDA, Bangkok, and are currently Assistant Teaching Professors at Aarhus Architecture School in Denmark.

Eduardo Cassina is an architect and urban sociologist trained in Glasgow, Porto, Amsterdam, Beijing, London and Moscow. In 2014 he co-founded METASITU, an urban collective devoted to the exploration of new spatial pedagogies. He currently teaches architecture at INDA, Chulalongkorn University in Bangkok. He co-runs an experimental architecture space, Monitor Lizard.

Tijn van de Wijdeven is an architect and educator experienced in design and research in relation to urban and architectural projects. He currently teaches at INDA, Chulalongkorn University in Bangkok where he was first year coordinator for several years amongst other roles. He also co-runs the experimental architecture space, Monitor Lizard.

Palimpsestia

When you enter the exhibition of Palimpsestia, you might rejoice in observing, and absorbing, the gargantuan amount of information that is available to you: models from previous editions are layered on top of each other, projectors of different ages casting images and shadows over a compound alloy of information panels, and scaled prototypes of different eras. This Biennial, unique among architectural events, remains a favorite among the cognoscenti: now on its twenty-third edition, Palimpsestia's only policy, and its most distinguishing feature, is that all of which is exhibited (models, projectors, television screens, and media players, and countless other devices that have been used by architects and curators to express ideas of space and light) remains wedged in their position, permanently. Curators and presenters of Palimpsestia have to address not only their curatorial pursuits but also the context in which they are presented.

The cumulative amalgamation of exhibition displays over the years is no stranger to the citizens of Palitomograd, the city that hosts Palimpsestia every two years, who, rather familiar with the amassing ambitions of the Biennial, have seen several of their municipal buildings overflow with exhibition paraphernalia. Worried that Palimpsestia would eventually submerge all of their old palaces, its disused arsenal, libraries, and museums, undercoats of architectural ideas and metaphors, the citizens of Palitomograd voiced their apprehension to the organizers. “What is a delightful archive of the evolution of architectural discourse, a wunderkammer of sorts, to some, is a hoarding threat to others”, I heard many of them repeat to any candid visitor who would listen.

The ever-growing abundance of accouterments, as well as the by-now nefarious dispute between the curators of the fifteenth and sixteenth editions (the former originally decided to erect a wall of polystyrene foam over the architectural effigies, prototypes, walls, ceilings, and floors, as if the remains of the previous editions had been covered by a shroud; whereas the latter decided to dissolve them with chemical glue to reveal once again the past regalia), triggered an organizational and cognitive shift, one of the most abrupt known in the history of Biennials: by the 18th edition, a distinct curatorial sentiment had been distilled, in contrast to previous paradigms of grandeur and flamboyant statements. From that juncture onwards, every Palimpsestia curator has been as attentive to the tenability of prospective editions, for all intents and purposes, as much as to their present one.

To this day, any visitor, whether an expert in Palladio or categorically unfamiliar with the world of architecture, is capable of observing the abundance of space-conscious works, as well as astute exhibition design solutions that support the circulation of visitors among new oeuvres, and their antecedents. Yet, as the years sum up, the sunsets and sunrises accumulate, and the dust settles, as any visitor would tell you, one is left to wonder how many more Palimpsestia editions are left, how many more exhibitions will there be space for?

Failed Biennials

By recycling the materials we use for the installations or exhibitions is also in some way recycling the past ideas and problems bringing them to light again. In this sense the scarcity of innovation and progressive thinking becomes obvious. Reusing the same concepts, materials and topics does not let us go beyond the margins and this practice somehow blocks the innovative and urgent critical thinking in us.
One exhibition follows the other and over and over again the same routine takes place. The organizing of a “Biennial” like event becomes a usual business for cultural sector, the city, the curator and everybody else involved in it.
One day will come when we will say it is enough when the overload of materials, permanently renewing events, new exhibitions, non-stop new information available for the audience transforms into an intellectual chaos. This tremendous amount of material cannot be perceived anymore as something important one can keep in his brain. We organize exhibitions about the environmental crisis, about our broken planet and at the same time produce so much garbage that we do exactly the same thing we are criticizing.
Enough is enough and the day will arrive when so much information will be converted into a big useless chaos and this practice of organizing Biennials will eventually fail. The question is how long can we live with all these different events offering one installation after another and one exhibition following the other? Does all these afford really bring us something important? Can the Biennials really act as catalysts for change? Who are the remaining caretakers when the “Biennial” is over? What sense does it really make to organize an overload of exhibitions every two years?
These questions keep bothering us and the various intellectuals in the architectural and art world start criticizing these kind of events and in many cases call them a waste of time, materials and money.
As the years pass and one edition of the Biennial follows the other in different parts of the world this idea of creating an event in such manner slowly becomes absurd. One might say that the concept of the “ Biennials” as we know it are already fading away. The progressive revival of the concept is urgently needed and anything else in our society “Biennials” should also reflect to the current environmental and urban state we live in.

Canonice

Never in all my travels had I ventured as far as the Triennale of Canonice. It was dusk when I landed there. At the entrance of the main venue, the assistant curator who welcomed me with the catalog in his hands, “the Architecture we have always Built”, resembled a professor who had taught me 30 years before and was dead. I was invited to tour the exhibition and, at the same time, to examine some old publications to understand how the show used to be: the same plans and sections in black and white instead of more saturated colours, a hand-carved timber model instead of gently CNC milled, another model to contemplate the coherence and poetry of a space instead of trendy collages. To not disappoint the curators, you must praise the old publications together with the current one, and you must be careful to contain your regret at the absence of any significant change: highlighting in your dialogues the eternal relevance of architectural fundamentals, the indisputable role of architecture drawings, the unquestionable methodologies of architectural design and the unmissable role of architectural references, you need to express a contained but visible excitement for the epidermal deviations from the canon, praising their foresight and the progeny of the canon in all the corners of the kingdom. If Canonice had remained unchanged because this is its own true skin or the mask it is convenient to wear, I don’t know, and nobody in the kingdom dares to guess.

A young architect was meticulously drawing the imperfection of a concrete wall as if it was an onyx slab from Madagascar, and I thought I recognized him; when I turned, he had disappeared down the corridor, but I realized that he looked like the assistant curator who, already old when I was a child, could no longer be among the living. I turned my gaze aside and I no longer dared look anyone in the face. I thought: "If Canonice is a triennial I am seeing in a dream, where you encounter only the dead, the dream frightens me. If Canonice is a real Triennale, showcasing the work of living people, I need only to continue looking at them and the resemblances will dissolve, alien faces will appear, bearing anguish. In either case, it is best for me not to insist on staring at them."

An elegant architect was scrupulously cleaning a diorama with miniature velvet curtains, to arrange it on a table together with other models of the same kind. He raised his face: he was Aldo Rossi. I thought: "You reach a moment in life when, among the people that inspire your work, the dead outnumber the living. And the mind refuses to accept new paradigms and new expressions: on every new work you start, it tries to print the old forms, to each one it finds the most suitable mask." Perhaps, to him, I also resemble someone who was dead. I had barely arrived at Canonice and I was already one of them, I had gone over to their side, absorbed in that kaleidoscope of line-weight, solid timber, plaster casts. I thought: "Perhaps Canonice is the Triennale where you arrive dying and where each finds again the masters he has known. This means I, too, am dead." And I also thought: "This means the beyond is not necessarily new."
