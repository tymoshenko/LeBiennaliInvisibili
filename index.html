The Mayor does not necessarily believe everything The Ambassador tells her when she describes the Biennials visited on her expeditions, but the Mayor does continue listening to the celebrated cultural advisor, and former curator, with greater attention and curiosity than she shows any other cultural attaché or UNESCO representative of hers. In the careers of leaders there is a moment which follows pride in the eclectic line up of the cultural pursuit we have exhibited, and the excitement and fulfillment of knowing we shall soon celebrate any thought of collecting and publicizing them. “Biennials can make or destroy your city, Ma’am” the Ambassador would warn “It is up to you, to choose wisely the alchemy of terms and accents of the gathering you want to create”.

There is a sense of contemplation that comes over us in the morning, with the sound of commuters after eight o’clock and the fumes of traf ic filling streets and squares; a fogginess that makes the buildings and neighborhoods blur on the semi-orthogonal grid of the horizon. From this horizon, a series of curators show up, one after the other, reporting to us the reviews of the last visiting critics, from libel to laudatory, and printouts of the articles of renowned artists who af irm our curatorial production, of ering in exchange periodic submissions of Biennial themes, budget proposals, contact sheets. A sea of tote bags, of memories of chance encounters, of air-kisses, of new names and new zeitgeists.

On this occasion, The Mayor has summoned the Ambassador to report on Biennials from far away lands to prepare her own Mayor’s Biennial. Not all biennials are the same. Neither will they return to being what they once were. However, the format of showcasing new work, triggering discussions, and letting new canons emerge, as a time-based event that takes place once every two years at the same location, has become the pilgrimage for the culture industries in the new millennium. Biennials - or biennales, a bastardization of the italian original term that has slipped into as many different languages as locations - not only have the power to forge discourse, but also transient communities united by gatherings every two or three years, and cities that are momentarily transformed and forever associated with those festivals of human culture.

Le Biennali Invisibili is a project that explores eleven imaginaries of such encounters, under the same lens that Italo Calvino created when describing alternative urban realities in his book Le città invisibili (Invisible Cities). Le Biennali Invisibili embraces fiction and pseudo-reality as a framework for curatorial pursuit, as a series of virtual events, proposing and critiquing the conventional celebration of the design & art community.

Le Biennali Invisibili is a project by animali domestici (Alicia Lazzaroni and Antonio Bernacchi), Tijn van de Wijdeven, and Eduardo Cassina. It is curated by Alfons Hug and commissioned by the Goethe-Zentrum Baku.

Alicia Lazzaroni and Antonio Bernacchi are the co-founders of Animali Domestici, a design practice with an active interest in non anthropocentric coexistence and ecology in its wider sense. They hold a postgraduate master from ETSAM UPM Madrid. After practicing a few years in Singapore, they became instructors and year coordinators in INDA, Bangkok, and are currently Assistant Teaching Professors at Aarhus Architecture School in Denmark.

Eduardo Cassina is an architect and urban sociologist trained in Glasgow, Porto, Amsterdam, Beijing, London and Moscow. In 2014 he co-founded METASITU, an urban collective devoted to the exploration of new spatial pedagogies. He currently teaches architecture at INDA, Chulalongkorn University in Bangkok. He co-runs an experimental architecture space, Monitor Lizard.

Tijn van de Wijdeven is an architect and educator experienced in design and research in relation to urban and architectural projects. He currently teaches at INDA, Chulalongkorn University in Bangkok where he was first year coordinator for several years amongst other roles. He also co-runs the experimental architecture space, Monitor Lizard.

Bottavia

In Bottavia, which includes contributions both from north and south, with installations, photography, models, artifacts, and so on, there is a script through which you can sense how the Biennial takes form. At first sight, nothing seems to resemble Bottavia’s Biennial less than the machinery that runs the script itself, racks of CPUs and wires whose patterns are repeated along straight and circular lines, interwoven with brilliantly coloured spires, in a repetition that can be followed throughout the whole system. But if you pause and examine its open code carefully, you become convinced that each operation performed by the script has its own objective in the almost infinite process of pre-selection of the artists, from literary anywhere, and all the things contained in the Biennial are related to some hidden affinity, arranged according to their somehow “true” relationship, which escapes your eye distracted by the bustle, the throngs, the shoving.

All of Bottavia’s confusion, the memes’ proliferation, the TikTok videos, the countless webpages is what is evident in the incomplete perspective you grasp; but the script proves that there is a point from which the Biennial shows its true proportions, the “neural” scheme implicit in its every, tiniest detail. It is easy to get lost in Bottavia’s contents: but when you concentrate and stare at the program’s giant leaflet, you recognize the installation you were seeking in a crimson or indigo or magenta thread which, in a wide loop, brings you to the purple enclosure that is your real destination. Every contributor of Bottavia compares the scripts' immobile order with his perspective of the art scene, an anguish of his own, and each can find, concealed among the arabesques, an answer, the story of his life, the twists of fate.

An AI geek was questioned about the mysterious bond between two objects so dissimilar as the structure of the script and its curatorial concept. One of the two objects -he replied- has the form the big data gave to the internet itself in which the worlds embody their virtual counterparts; the other is an approximate reflection, like any automatic creation. For some time the augurs had been sure that the script's harmonious machine learning routines were of a new generation that the world had yet to understand. The geek was interpreted in this sense, arousing no controversy. But you could, similarly, come to the opposite conclusion: that a truly unbiased map of the universe is the Biennial of Bottavia, just as it is, a stain that spreads out shapelessly, with crooked links, contents that crumble one upon the other amid clouds of data, streams, screams in the darkness.


Conversation
  between Merve Bedir
  and Bager Akbay

Merve Bedir is an architect based in Hong Kong. She works at the intersection of the technologies of labour, landscape, mobility, and commons. https://www.aformalacademy.com/ & http://landandcc.com/
Bager Akbay is an artist-as-researcher based in Istanbul.

Merve Bedir:
I research the
automation of labor
in the factories of Pearl River Delta region, as well as an unrealized 2018 Hong Kong Biennale exhibition proposal called
"Collective Intelligence",
where we wanted to research the coalescing forms of intelligence among peoples and nonhumans. For this conversation with you as a joint contribution to the Invisible Biennale exhibition, my interests were your former works "Deniz Yılmaz" (bot poet) and "Shepherd" (bot curator). Shepherd, especially, as a metaphor for the curator fascinates me. Shepherd is the one who cares and is responsible for the herd.


Bager Akbay:

The shepherd looks after the herd, but also the one who leads it. Prophets were almost always shepherds before becoming, some politicians describe their former work as being shepherds, in Ancient Greece philosophers were not allowed to engage in any profession but could only do shepherding. Curating is as such a hierarchical position. The curator might be creating your identity while caring for you, or it might be creating space for you to flourish.


Merve:
In the factory, there are three roles, the blue collar, the white collar, and the boss. In our research we found that human labour is still preferred to robots, while bots used for operation replace the human middle manager (white collar labour) in the workplace.


Bager:
In fact, it is labour that changes form. The blue collar becomes the robot, the white collar becomes the operation bot. I'm curious about the form and nature of the boss, the leader (in our case, the curator). A cooperative works on three kinds of charters, initiating, operation, and decommissioning. If you include the other two charters, the operation charter could change towards leadership. Here, the leader is the contract, the operation, the code. If the codes are open, if the human language of the protocols are clear/ can be identified, and if this language can be decided by the people, that's a good exhibition, or a real society.


Merve:
It's the value systems that define the codes, isn't it? On the one hand the value systems define the characteristics of the curator, and on the other, the curator (singular or collective) defines the value of an art work, how important/significant that work is, and also how much it’s worth.


Bager:
Curator is like a cell's membrane, it takes the input from its participants and reflects the output back to them, or to outside. Similarly, leaders reflect the characteristics of their societies. In "Shepherd" my idea was to leave all decision making to the bot curator, as the cell's membrane. I started forms for different jobs regarding an exhibition in a Human Resources website, people applied to it for the different positions in the exhibition, such as defining a concept sentence for the exhibition, a workflow, commissions, translations, and so on. There was an open call, artists applied, and the process was completely organized according to the open code of the bot.

My point is that humans are adaptation machines. If you put a human in a society it will take the form of the society. The form of society is political, which is shaped by laws and the cultural codes. If we change the rules of the law, the society inside will change. A company works with its own operational codes. It can act with different values in different parts of the code/algorithm, for instance it could be more communist in human resources, while being liberal in distributing profit. We need to see that these codes are political. If we can analyze what is good or bad in these codes, what works better in which way, the differences of the governance models, etc. then we can also see what mode requires what quality, what problem requires what control mechanisms, and so on. These codes and modes need to be open and developed based on consensus.


Merve:

In the laws of the cooperatives (or companies), we still don't/won't have the initiation and decommissioning as bots, though. "Shepherd" is an inspiration in that sense, that this is a possibility in governance.


Bager:
Governance is a self-conscious mechanism/system, a matter of cybernetics. We can understand the laws of a system only by being self-conscious, the robots can work without being self-conscious, which is a problem, but the bots can, to a certain extent, which is interesting. In "Shepherd," the applicants, who declared they can curate the exhibition, cross-voted among themselves about who should really do it. At every step there was such a self-control process for qualification and consensus. The exhibition as a process and outcome was so natural and so smooth that no one realized anything. This is striking because this is also how things happen in the real world.


Merve:
Well, Constant suggested that in future all blue-collar work will be done by robots and humans will become architects. But, I feel you think the opposite, as humans should/will actually continue blue-collar work, while codes/algorithms become leaders.


Bager:
Which mode do you think is closer to human nature? Think of a mayor, how can they govern one million people, right? It just doesn't make sense. I'm thinking we should be doing more blue-collar work, and then transparent, open-code algorithms (rules, laws) could lead.


Merve:
But historically, the leader has always been outside the law.


Bager:
Yes, that's people not leaving the decision to the existing law, but creating exceptions. That's why governments love crises, because this gives them the possibility of suspending the law, and continuing their sovereignty. Think about The DAO in France, with the recent crisis they had, if they let it to the algorithm, The DAO should have shut down, but they changed the algorithm and continued. When the leader is the algorithm, there are no exceptions and no bad leaders. If the code is open, the leader will be open and transformed, too. We criticize our leaders but we do the exact same things in our lives.


Merve:

Same goes for curating.


Bager:
Exactly. Essentially, people should discuss and decide on the code, and the code should be organizing how to continuously discuss. The curator is this code. Then, art is doing what you see, and what you want to see. This attitude is not included in the scope of other disciplines anymore. That's why I'm interested in art. This is why I am working on Mahlas now.


Merve:
I want to go back to the question of consciousness for a last note. Before, we talked about the self-consciousness of an algorithm and society. This really makes me think of the 2019 Hong Kong movement. What happened in Hong Kong was this self-consciousness of a movement, and utilizing open code software as tools to help the movement of this self-consciousness. We know this worked because the same consciousness also has led to a success of managing the pandemic in the city. To witness the possibility of a leaderless governance in this example is mind blowing.


Bager:
It is about how we define the leader, and the algorithm/ the bot. The leader and the algorithm are nothing but contracts. They are rules and regulations. Who we are, is who our workers, managers, curators, or leaders are. As I said before, what we need to make sure is to keep the source code open and transparent, discuss how it's run. Also we need to diversify the tools, and the decision making mechanisms based on issues. The algorithm you use for one issue may not be suitable for another. There cannot be one formula, one answer to every question.
Canonice

Never in all my travels had I ventured as far as the Triennale of Canonice. It was dusk when I landed there. At the entrance of the main venue, the assistant curator who welcomed me with the catalog in his hands, “the Architecture we have always Built”, resembled a professor who had taught me 30 years before and was dead. I was invited to tour the exhibition and, at the same time, to examine some old publications to understand how the show used to be: the same plans and sections in black and white instead of more saturated colours, a hand-carved timber model instead of gently CNC milled, another model to contemplate the coherence and poetry of a space instead of trendy collages. To not disappoint the curators, you must praise the old publications together with the current one, and you must be careful to contain your regret at the absence of any significant change: highlighting in your dialogues the eternal relevance of architectural fundamentals, the indisputable role of architecture drawings, the unquestionable methodologies of architectural design and the unmissable role of architectural references, you need to express a contained but visible excitement for the epidermal deviations from the canon, praising their foresight and the progeny of the canon in all the corners of the kingdom. If Canonice had remained unchanged because this is its own true skin or the mask it is convenient to wear, I don’t know, and nobody in the kingdom dares to guess.

A young architect was meticulously drawing the imperfection of a concrete wall as if it was an onyx slab from Madagascar, and I thought I recognized him; when I turned, he had disappeared down the corridor, but I realized that he looked like the assistant curator who, already old when I was a child, could no longer be among the living. I turned my gaze aside and I no longer dared look anyone in the face. I thought: "If Canonice is a triennial I am seeing in a dream, where you encounter only the dead, the dream frightens me. If Canonice is a real Triennale, showcasing the work of living people, I need only to continue looking at them and the resemblances will dissolve, alien faces will appear, bearing anguish. In either case, it is best for me not to insist on staring at them."

An elegant architect was scrupulously cleaning a diorama with miniature velvet curtains, to arrange it on a table together with other models of the same kind. He raised his face: he was Aldo Rossi. I thought: "You reach a moment in life when, among the people that inspire your work, the dead outnumber the living. And the mind refuses to accept new paradigms and new expressions: on every new work you start, it tries to print the old forms, to each one it finds the most suitable mask." Perhaps, to him, I also resemble someone who was dead. I had barely arrived at Canonice and I was already one of them, I had gone over to their side, absorbed in that kaleidoscope of line-weight, solid timber, plaster casts. I thought: "Perhaps Canonice is the Triennale where you arrive dying and where each finds again the masters he has known. This means I, too, am dead." And I also thought: "This means the beyond is not necessarily new."

Today, many agree that the biennales have become obsolete, even though currently there is no other format comparable in its prominence. Recently, every corner of the world has wanted to place itself on the map of modern art tourism by way of participation in the Biennale. It was vitally important for every artist to be included in the group of participants, despite the awareness of the hypocrisy of the corporate-managerial elite, which runs the contemporary artistic system, and its political correctness, which limits individual freedom of creative expression.

In the era of universal automation, sovereign Internet, trade wars, and other signs of the times, the price for international megaprojects has become too high for organizers increasingly doubting the need to pay for their own critique. The biennales used to be a compromise and are even more so now, during the recession of the biennale boom, although it is still they who define the progressive social and aesthetic agenda in contemporary world culture.

A modern man, freed from the shackles before the individualistic society, which both limited and provided him with security and peace, did not acquire freedom in terms of realization of his personality. In most cases, a person is not yet mature enough to be completely independent and objective. The cultural and social crisis of our days is reflected directly in the meaning of freedom for a modern person, as well as on the problem of interconnection and interaction between psychological and sociological factors of social development.

The new history was conditioned by efforts aimed at acquiring freedom from the political, economic, and spiritual shackles that bound a person aimed at victory over the forces of totalitarianism. Despite numerous defeats, freedom usually prevailed. History has confirmed that a person can control himself, make decisions by himself, think, and feel as he deems right.

It is paradoxical that although freedom brings a person independence and rationality of his own existence, it simultaneously isolates him, awakening in him a feeling of powerlessness and anxiety. This isolation is unbearable, and a person is faced with a choice: to either get rid of freedom with the help of a new dependence, new submission, or grow to the full realization of positive freedom based on the uniqueness and individuality of each.

If we consider the problem of a person and his freedom and lack of freedom and imagine that space is freedom, while the constraint of space is non-freedom, then the image of a wall that ascends in its functional basis to an absolute forbiddance is precisely what represents a system of prohibitions in the social structure. The horizon, however, can be viewed as an image of experience - contours, and outlines that are not inherent in objects in reality but traced in them by our perception, brought into the world by our finite presence. Any experience, in its most important feature, reveals the property of finitude, which means that experience always has boundaries. Since these boundaries are not laid down once and for all but are shifting, changeable, they form horizons.

Suppose an image could no longer stand on its own, but would have to conform to a larger narrative structure. Using text to contextualize the image is one possibility; another is to construct a series of images in a way that forms a visual argument. The combination of both is perhaps the surest way to do so. Take, for instance, the photo-essay “Before They Disappear” by Walker Evans from 1957: a series of thirteen close-ups of logos printed on freight cars, each captioned with the design date of these “brave and naive emblems,” as he calls them in a short introductory text. Steeped in nostalgia yet acutely aware of the corporate present, Evans’ text does not only mourn the day when this signage will disappear from the American landscape but also anchors historical typography in a cultural context. Its cumulative effect withdraws the spectator’s attention from a visual attraction to aesthetic abstraction, and instead orients the mind to further contemplation on the state of the nation.
The combination of text and image in a progressive sequence is thus a form of resistance to photography’s transformation from utility to commodity. In his critique of the politics of representation, photographer Allan Sekula argues that text can “anchor, contradict, reinforce, subvert, complement, particularize, or go beyond the meanings offered by the images themselves” (1978). In this way, a picture would not remain a spectacle of lines, colors, and composition, but a critical document of society. The formats of the reportage, photo-essay, or travelogue are archetypal examples of mechanisms of representation that transfer photos and words from the wall to the page. They draw our attention from the visual to the visible, and from the aesthetics of the sign to the meaning of the signified.

However modern this formula may seem, the combination of image, text, and narrative extends a Millenium back to the devotional culture of Medieval Europe. In a society obsessed with sin and remission, devotional manuscripts were a popular device for private worship. These meditational guidebooks were illustrated with episodes from the life of Christ by fragmenting the Scriptural narrative into frames that could be clearly represented and perceived. In addition to the visual, each page was annotated with affective prayers, psalms, and hymns, anchoring the illustrations in religious practice. This didactic coupling of image and language is meant to negate the danger of sensorial attraction to form in favor of achieving the next stage of devotion which is concentration and meditation. The linearity of the manuscript, with its steady progression of the narrative and intensification of emotions, makes it possible for the pious believer to follow all stages of the Passion in order to ultimately arrive at Christ’s resurrection—and their own salvation.

This representation of the Passion is then a travelogue par excellence, a journey evolving through space and time. In this composition, every element is crucial for the internal logic of the devotional apparatus: the order of images and affectivity of the annotations influence the legibility of the narrative. In this algorithmic condition the syntactic become prominent: an image, like a word, only makes sense vis-à-vis the whole. It was a disciplinary tool that normalizes the relationship between image and beholder, where a coherency was contingent not only on the bond between image and annotation but also between the sequence and its parts. In other words, while the fusion between sign and signified begins to wear off, the prominence of the sequence naturalizes the condition in which the relational triumphs the autonomous.
















The Stations of the Cross ( the Via Crucis, or Via Dolorosa) is one of the most common rituals in the Catholic Church. It is formed of a sequence of numbered stations that represent the Passion: the sentencing, crucifixion, and entombment of Jesus Christ. In this peripatetic mode of devotion, the ​station is instrumental as it sets the rhythm of a ritual through an unraveling narrative deployed within a church, a religious complex, a sacred landscape, or the urban.















Each canonical station contains a representation of the Christological event, staged like a scene in a play, whose individual scenes are easier to grasp than the totality of the event. The mode of representation varies from paintings, bass-reliefs, or, as in the case of the Sacred Mountains of northern Italy— colorful life-sized tableaux vivants created with theatrical realism. This tangible representation was popular in its intensity and clarity; distant from being subtle or stoic, it catered to all audiences.






















The Station is a paradox: it marks a stop in order to perpetuate movement. And yet it is crucial: as a violent event full of tragedy and drama, a reenactment of the Passion must be controlled by choreographing the narrative through a serial progression of emotions. From original sin to personal salvation, the stations must be erected with a physical interval apart, and yet they must be available to follow as a complete devotional sequence.















The secluded mountain-top, where staged scenes took place inside sequenced chapels, was imprinted on the minds of the pilgrims. In time, repetition, and ritual, the images would be constructed in the minds - on the basis of memories of things seen. The legibility of narrative and clarity of order allowed its transportation to other places ( with typological variations), even to those surrounding a pilgrim’s own home.














While the didactic content of the Stations was imprinted into spatial memories, the Via Crucis lost its representational value, becoming more about the linearity of the path than the anecdotal destination. This serialization and optimization of the Way of the Cross resulted in the complete codification of Christ’s public execution. It thus restrained and disciplined not only of representations and movement, but also religious behavior and pious imagination.























The systematization of the Stations and crystallization of the path allowed its replicability across Europe. Finally, it was imported to Jerusalem itself, where the Stational ritual was born but never fully realized. There, in Christ’s city, nothing remained but numbers on old-new walls. Overlaid on a city fabric where religions have coexisted for millennia, the Passionate route remains but a floating sign amongst the city’s many.

Rexia

No artistic gathering has a more obscure inception story than that of the Biennial of Rexia, the most ancient of cultural events known to humans. It is no surprise that its origins remain a mystery to many. Everyone concurs that its first edition was nine hundred thirty-two years ago, for there is enough evidence for that claim, but what attendees often seem to forget is that it commenced as a religious pilgrimage event, where artisans from the region of Nackchi, where Rexia took place, would flock to flaunt, flash and emblazon their latest creations and crafts smothered in devotional fervor about God, or the Prophet’s life. Over the centuries, as winds -and their names- changed, earthquakes shivered the Earth, and civilizations rose and fell, Rexia had naturally evolved into an agnostic artistic event, desacralized and emancipated from any religious trace.

If a facet that has remained untouched throughout time was to be mentioned, scholars would agree that such an immutable element would be the election ceremony of its main, and sole, curator; a figure directly appointed by the curator of the previous edition. This promulgation, known as Denuntiatio Ad Regime, the auspices of which have been known to travelers and locals, cognoscenti and illiterates, occurs, since time immemorial, the day after the aperture of the Biennial. In front of all attendees, among all the fervency and zeal, the current curator solemnly proclaims the name of the curator for the next edition. The secrecy around the promulgation, as well as the anticipation, have historically triggered tremendously emotional scenes in the audience, often followed by a profound murmuration of gossip and commentary, muffed by clapping, cheer, and, at times, gasps.

The position of Chief Curator of Rexia, the pinnacle of absolute curatorial virtues, is coveted by curators from all cardinals points, eager to enter the cannons of History of Art. In its four hundred sixty-six editions, the position has only been rejected on one occurrence. The unabridged power characterizing the role of Chief Curator of Rexia, a position like no other, where appointees do not have to answer to anyone but their ambitions, where budgets know no limits, and infrastructure is of the highest quality known, often turns events into largely dramatic, yet often disjointed, monuments to their custodian’s egos. For this reason, no critic has ever considered Rexia an influential event.

Yet, if I must speak to you about its importance, it is not its repercussion, but on its archive, that one must consider. Rexia has a library whose contents have been intensely studied by scholars, collectors, and artists, containing all physical, and, more recently, digital, documentation of every single precedent edition. Ancient parchments and scrolls coexist with unsold catalogs, samples of posters, lost tote bags, and exhibition brochures with curatorial statements, all of which are archived in the most rigorous chronological order. This tremendous archive has matured into a pilgrimage destination known to all in the art world, the visit of which remains a rite of passage that everyone who calls themself artist, curator, or historian, must, at least once in their lifetime, complete. The same fate, however, is not shared by the Biennial exhibition per se, which has been perceived, for at least the past three centuries, if not earlier, as an ephemeral addendum whose only paramountcy is the Denuntiatio Ad Regime ceremony on the second day of the event. There are no official visitor statistics of the exhibition, for they would disclose this mournful truth. You might face difficulty looking for people who cherish the actual event when it is happening, as most do so retrospectively when it can be studied and dissected from the printed elements left behind, within the walls of the library; when it can be observed in connection to a larger sequence of editions that came afore and after it.

Art philomaths are delighted to confabulate theories around the curatorial shifts, the desacralization of the event, the discursive tensions that have emerged between different editions, or, a personal favorite, the conspiracy around the appointments. Some particular editions have been absorbed into the art historians’ canons ecumenical, such as those when curators had misread the intentions of their heirs resulting in Biennials of antagonistic persuasions, or when for certain periods nepotism and partisanship affected the appointments for Main Curators for several editions in a row, turning Rexia into a stale event. These episodes, as any reputable art historian would confirm, have been normally followed by a series of decidedly surprising, and chaotic editions. The cumulative succession of turns -often good, at times detrimental-, the lamentable decisions, the expected and unexpected results, and the manifestation of curatorial egos stretched over the elephantine existence of the event, were the main attractions of Rexia. Regardless of the unexpected shifts, its continuation remains certain: if I were to speak of what ascertains the survival of Rexia, is the resolute compliance and reverence to the Main Curator’s orders, no matter who they are, or how delirious their wishes present to be.

Many people tried eagerly to be part of the Rexia-mission, knowing very well, that landing on Rexia, finding the right place for the works of art, and following the last curatorial orders would be the last thing in their lives. No one would survive on Rexia, but after all, no one would survive on earth. Of course, there were only a small crew needed, so in order to become at least a spiritual member of the mission, people began to study the history and the theory of art, they acclaimed (or did not so) the work of the SUPER-KURATORS and they hated the critics, especially those, who denied the deep cultural and (more or less) religious sense of the Rexia-mission. The archives of art were intensely studied by scholars, collectors, and artists, containing all physical and digital documentation of every single precedent step by the SUPER-KURATORS and their explanations, both in the historical and the Ästhetik context. Art was the new - and last religion. Art was the new - and last ideology. Art was the new - and last reason to live. People gathered in the archives. Ancient parchments and scrolls there coexisted with unsold catalogs, samples of posters, lost tote bags, and exhibition brochures with curatorial statements, all of which are archived around the world in very different orders. But the tremendous archives had matured into a pilgrimage destination known to all, not only in the art world, and the visits to every kind of art archive, museum, library, collection emerged into a new rite of passage, the great farewell in the name of Rexia. Once before the big explosion, nearly everyone (including those who wouldn’t give a damn at the beginning of the Rexia-mission) must perform such a rite: Being part of the SUPER-MISSION: Man will die, but art will survive.

The same fate, however, is not shared by the mission of the Last Biennial itself, which had perceived, more and more, for the last hundred days, if not longer, as an ephemeral addendum whose only paramountcy is the Denuntio ad Fatum Arts-ceremony and the inauguration of the SUPER-KURATOR every now and then. There are no official statistics about the real knowledge of the works of art chosen for the Rexia-mission, for they would disclose this mournful truth. The new religious art had no real content. You might face difficulty looking for people who cherish the actual event of the mission and how it is happening, as most do so prospectively, when it could be studied and dissected from the broadcasted elements left behind, within the circuits of followers and friends of Rexia. The art-religion of the Rexia-mission were both, fundamentalistic and superficial.

The small group of art philomaths however were delighted to confabulate theories around the curatorial shifts, the strange sacralization of the mission, the discursive tensions that had emerged between different regimes of the SUPER-KURATORS, or, a personal favorite, the conspiracy around the appointments. Some particular “chapters“ have been absorbed into the narration of the end as ecumenical (fate or guilt?) such as those when SUPER-KURATORS had misread the intentions of their heirs resulting in decisions of antagonistic persuasions, or when for certain periods nepotism and partisanship affected the appointments for SUPER-KURATORS for a certain time, turning the Rexia-mission into a stale event (including declining “likes“ and followers). These episodes, as even any reputable art critic would confirm, had been normally followed by a series of decidedly surprising and chaotic curatorial politics. "This you call a masterpiece?“ We heard some people gasp (and "whataboutism“ got a new meaning). The cumulative succession of turns - often good, at times detrimental -, the lamentable decisions, the expected and unexpected results, and the manifestation of curatorial egos stretched over the elephantine existence of the mission, were the main attractions of Rexia. Regardless of the unexpected shifts, its continuation remained certain: If I were to speak of what ascertains the survival of the Rexia-mission till the end, ist the resolute compliance and reverence to the SUPER-KURATORS orders, no matter who they were, or how delirious their wishes presented to be.

When the Rexia-mission started, taking 100 000 works of art to post-human eternal life, it left us not only with some kind of transcendental hope but also with a new concept of leadership. Three days after the WARHOLA 1 started its flight, an Icelandic scientist discovered a bug in the algorithm that predicted the end of the world. The big explosion is canceled. Mankind will live on. If only without the 100 000 masterpieces of art, which will live on Rexia, without dust, without humidity, without wind, and without life. Forever and ever and ever and ever and ever…
